### 2.1 Definition and Characteristics of Nanoparticles

Nanoparticles (NPs) refer to particles with at least one dimension in the size range of 1 to 100 nanometers. They exhibit distinct physical and chemical properties compared to macroscopic particles, which endow them with unique advantages in drug delivery. The main characteristics of nanoparticles include high surface area, unique optical and electronic properties, excellent biocompatibility, and superior drug loading capacity.

**Surface Area**: Nanoparticles possess a high surface area, which means that per unit mass, they have more surface regions available for drug molecule adsorption and interactions with biological molecules.

**Optical Properties**: Nanoparticles can exhibit unique optical properties, such as Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR), which make them promising for applications in photothermal therapy and optical imaging.

**Electronic Properties**: The electronic properties of nanoparticles depend on their composition and structure. For example, gold nanoparticles (Au NPs) exhibit good electronic conductivity, suitable for the development of electronic sensors.

**Biocompatibility**: The biocompatibility of nanoparticles is a critical factor in their application in drug delivery. Good biocompatibility reduces the irritation and toxicity to biological tissues.

**Drug Loading Capacity**: Nanoparticles can load drugs through physical encapsulation, chemical bonding, or cavities within the nanoparticles, enhancing the stability and bioavailability of drugs.

### 2.2 Basic Principles of Drug Delivery

The basic principle of drug delivery systems is to ensure that drugs are effectively delivered to target tissues or cells to achieve optimal therapeutic effects. The key roles of nanoparticles in drug delivery are highlighted in several aspects:

**Targeted Delivery**: Nanoparticles can achieve targeted delivery to specific cells or tissues through physical adsorption, chemical modification, or biomolecule-guided pathways, significantly improving local drug concentrations and reducing systemic side effects.

**Controlled Release**: By controlling the structure and composition of nanoparticles, drug release can be achieved at a slow rate within the body, maintaining a steady concentration of drugs and extending the duration of action.

**Protection of Drugs**: Nanoparticles can protect drugs from degradation and inactivation by enzymes and pH environments within the body.

**Enhancement of Bioavailability**: Nanoparticles can increase the solubility and dispersibility of drugs, thereby enhancing their bioavailability and more effective entry into the bloodstream.

### 2.3 Working Principles of Nanoparticles in Drug Delivery

The working principles of nanoparticles in drug delivery can be summarized in the following steps:

**1. Encapsulation of Drugs**: Drugs are encapsulated within nanoparticles through physical encapsulation, chemical bonding, or cavities within the nanoparticles.

**2. Transport of Drugs**: Encapsulated nanoparticles are administered into the body via intravenous injection, oral administration, or other delivery methods and transported through the bloodstream to target tissues or cells.

**3. Targeted Drug Release**: Under specific physiological conditions (such as pH, temperature, enzyme activity, etc.), nanoparticles release drugs, achieving local or systemic therapy.

**4. Metabolism of Released Drugs**: After drug release, the released drugs are cleared through the body's metabolic pathways.

### 2.4 Advantages of Nanoparticles in Drug Delivery

Nanoparticles in drug delivery offer the following advantages:

**1. Improvement of Therapeutic Efficacy**: Nanoparticles can significantly increase drug concentrations in the body, thereby enhancing therapeutic effects.

**2. Reduction of Side Effects**: Through targeted delivery, nanoparticles can deliver drugs directly to pathological tissues, reducing damage to normal tissues.

**3. Control of Drug Release**: Nanoparticles can control the rate of drug release, achieving sustained or demand-driven release of drugs.

**4. Improvement of Drug Stability**: Nanoparticles can protect drugs from the influence of the internal environment of the body, enhancing drug stability.

**5. Enhancement of Bioavailability**: Nanoparticles can increase the solubility and dispersibility of drugs, thereby improving their bioavailability and more effective entry into the bloodstream.

### 2.5 Challenges of Nanoparticles in Drug Delivery

Despite the tremendous potential of nanoparticles in drug delivery, their application still faces some challenges:

**1. Biocompatibility**: Ensuring the good biocompatibility of nanoparticles to reduce irritation and toxicity to biological tissues.

**2. Targeted Delivery**: Achieving precise targeting of nanoparticles to specific cells or tissues to minimize off-target delivery of drugs.

**3. Drug Release**: Controlling the rate of drug release from nanoparticles to meet different therapeutic needs.

**4. Production Cost**: Reducing the production cost of nanoparticles to make them more economically viable for clinical applications.

Nanoparticles offer unique advantages in drug delivery, such as improving therapeutic efficacy, reducing side effects, and controlling drug release. 零级释放模型假设药物以恒定的速率释放，与药物浓度无关。其数学公式为：
