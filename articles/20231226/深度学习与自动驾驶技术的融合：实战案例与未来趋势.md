自动驾驶技术是近年来 fastest-growing field in artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML). It has attracted significant attention from both academia and industry. The main goal of autonomous driving is to enable vehicles to navigate safely and efficiently without human intervention. To achieve this, a variety of sensors, such as cameras, lidar, radar, and GPS, are used to collect data about the vehicle's environment. This data is then processed by advanced algorithms to make decisions about steering, acceleration, and braking.

## 1.1 Brief History of Autonomous Driving

The concept of autonomous vehicles has been around for over a century. In the early 1900s, engineers and inventors began to explore the possibility of creating self-driving cars. However, it was not until the 21st century that the technology began to advance rapidly.

In 2004, the DARPA Grand Challenge was held, which was a major milestone in the development of autonomous driving technology. The challenge was to have autonomous vehicles complete a 150-mile off-road course in the Mojave Desert. Although none of the vehicles were able to complete the course, the competition spurred significant interest and investment in the field.

Since then, many companies and research institutions have been working on the development of autonomous driving technology. In 2010, Google's self-driving car project was launched, which has since become a leading force in the field. Other major players in the autonomous driving industry include Tesla, Uber, and Baidu.

## 1.2 Current State of Autonomous Driving Technology

Today, autonomous driving technology is still in its early stages of development. Most self-driving cars on the market today are considered Level 2 or Level 3 vehicles, according to the Society of Automotive Engineers (SAE) classification system.

Level 2 vehicles require the driver to maintain control of the vehicle at all times, while Level 3 vehicles allow the driver to cede control to the autonomous system under certain conditions.

Despite the progress that has been made, there are still many challenges that need to be addressed before fully autonomous vehicles can become a reality. These challenges include:

- **Safety**: Ensuring that autonomous vehicles can operate safely in all conditions is a top priority. This includes not only the vehicle's ability to avoid accidents, but also its ability to protect occupants in the event of a crash.
- **Legislation and regulation**: As autonomous vehicles become more common, new laws and regulations will need to be developed to govern their use. This includes everything from liability in the event of an accident to the requirements for testing and certification.
- **Infrastructure**: Autonomous vehicles will require significant changes to the existing transportation infrastructure. This includes everything from updated traffic signals and road signs to new communication systems for vehicle-to-vehicle (V2V) and vehicle-to-infrastructure (V2I) communication.
- **Public acceptance**: For autonomous vehicles to become widely adopted, they will need to gain the trust of the general public. This will require not only demonstrating the safety and reliability of the technology, but also addressing concerns about job displacement and privacy.

Despite these challenges, the potential benefits of autonomous driving technology are significant. These benefits include:

- **Reduced traffic congestion**: Autonomous vehicles can be more efficiently managed and coordinated, which could lead to a significant reduction in traffic congestion.
- **Improved fuel efficiency**: Autonomous vehicles can be optimized for fuel efficiency, which could lead to significant reductions in greenhouse gas emissions.
- **Increased accessibility**: Autonomous vehicles can provide mobility options for people who are unable to drive, such as the elderly and disabled.
- **Reduced accidents**: Autonomous vehicles have the potential to significantly reduce the number of accidents on the road, saving lives and reducing costs.
