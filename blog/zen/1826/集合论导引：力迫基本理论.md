### 2.2 力迫的定义与原理

力迫（Forcing）是保罗·科恩（Paul Cohen）在20世纪40年代提出的一种集合论技术，用于证明某些集合论命题的独立性，如连续统假设（Continuum Hypothesis, CH）。力迫的基本思想是通过引入一个“力”（forcing poset）来扩展原始模型，从而产生一个新模型，在新模型中，原始命题的真假可能发生变化。

- **力（Force）**：力是一个部分有序集合，其中每个元素代表一种可能的选择，这些选择能够改变原始模型的性质。力通常表示为 \(P\)，其元素为 \(\{p_i\}\)。
- **力迫（Forcing）**：力迫是一个从原始模型 \(M\) 到力 \(P\) 的映射，使得 \(M\) 的每个元素在 \(P\) 中有一个对应。这种映射通常称为“良好扩展”（good extension），它必须满足以下条件：
  - **正则性（Regularity）**：如果 \(p\) 是 \(P\) 中一个非终元素，那么在 \(M\) 中，所有与 \(p\) 相关的元素都是正则的。
  - **饱和性（Saturatedness）**：对于 \(M\) 中的任何公式 \(\phi\)，如果它在 \(P\) 的某个子集 \(Q\) 中被满足，那么它也在 \(M\) 中被满足。

### 2.3 力迫与连续统假设

连续统假设是一个关于实数集合 \( \mathbb{R} \) 的基数（即集合的大小）的命题，它声称不存在任何实数集合的基数大于自然数集 \( \mathbb{N} \) 的基数，且小于实数集 \( \mathbb{R} \) 的基数。连续统假设的符号表示为 \(2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_1\)，其中 \( \aleph_0 \) 是 \( \mathbb{N} \) 的基数，\( \aleph_1 \) 是 \( \mathbb{R} \) 的基数。

力迫在证明连续统假设的独立性中发挥了关键作用。通过构造一个包含连续统假设否定（即存在一个集合的基数在 \( \aleph_0 \) 和 \( \aleph_1 \) 之间）的力迫模型，科恩证明了连续统假设既不能被证明也不能被反证。

Forcing, introduced by Paul Cohen in the 1940s, is a technique in set theory that was used to prove the independence of certain set-theoretical propositions, such as the Continuum Hypothesis (CH). The basic idea of forcing is to introduce a "force" (forcing poset) that extends the original model, thereby creating a new model where the truth or falsehood of a primitive proposition may change.

- **Force (P)**: A force is a partially ordered set where each element represents a possible choice that can change the properties of the original model. Forces are typically denoted by \(P\) with elements \(\{p_i\}\).
- **Forcing**:
  - A forcing relation is a mapping from an original model \(M\) to a force \(P\) such that each element of \(M\) has a corresponding element in \(P\).
  - This mapping is often referred to as a "good extension" and must satisfy the following conditions:
    - **Regularity**: If \(p\) is a non-final element of \(P\), then all elements related to \(p\) in \(M\) are regular.
    - **Saturatedness**: For any formula \(\phi\) in \(M\), if \(\phi\) is satisfied in a subset \(Q\) of \(P\), then it is also satisfied in \(M\).

### 2.3 Forcing and the Continuum Hypothesis

The Continuum Hypothesis (CH) is a proposition concerning the cardinality of the set of real numbers \(\mathbb{R}\). It asserts that there is no set whose cardinality is greater than that of the set of natural numbers \(\mathbb{N}\) and less than that of the set of real numbers \(\mathbb{R}\). The symbol for CH is \(2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_1\), where \(\aleph_0\) is the cardinality of \(\mathbb{N}\) and \(\aleph_1\) is the cardinality of \(\mathbb{R}\).

Forcing played a crucial role in proving the independence of the Continuum Hypothesis. Through constructing a forcing model that includes the negation of the CH (i.e., there exists a set with a cardinality between \(\aleph_0\) and \(\aleph_1\)), Cohen demonstrated that the CH is neither provable nor refutable within the standard set-theoretical framework.

### 2.4 Applications of Forcing in Model Theory

Model theory is a branch of mathematics that studies mathematical structures and their proof theories. Forcing has widespread applications in model theory, particularly in studying the independence of logical propositions.

- **Model Independence**:
  - The independence of a proposition refers to the fact that it cannot be proven nor disproven within a given model.
  - Forcing provides a powerful tool for studying model independence because it allows us to construct different models and explore the truth or falsehood of different logical propositions.

- **Forcing and Logical Theorems**:
  - Forcing can be used to prove the independence of certain logical theorems. For example, forcing proved the independence of the Axiom of Choice and the Continuum Hypothesis.

- **Forcing and Infinite Axioms**:
  - Forcing can also be used to study the properties of infinite sets, including the properties of infinite order and the relationships between infinite cardinals.

Through the introduction of these core concepts and connections, we can see the significant role that forcing plays in set theory and model theory and how it alters our understanding of set theory and logical propositions.
